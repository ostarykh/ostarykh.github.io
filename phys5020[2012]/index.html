Course Description: There will be little lecturing. The class time will be mostly used for interactive problem solving involving the entire class or groups of students. In order to benefit from the course it is very important to do the assigned reading prior to the classes. Quizzes are going to be used in the beginning of each Wednesday class to monitor student's preparedness for meaningful classroom discussions. Quizzes will consist of 3 multiple choice/conceptual questions to be answered in about 5 (or less!) minutes. If you are late to class, you will miss the quiz (and forfeit 5 possible points). Homeworks will feature the type of problems previously addressed in class.

Note that no late tests and quizzes are allowed (unless a documented emergency occurred) - if you are absent for a legitimate reason please contact the instructor in advance to take a test at an earlier date.

Homeworks are due on Wednesdays at 9:40 am (before the start of the class).

Taking the final test is mandatory for passing this course. Final test will be held on Monday May 1 from 8-10 am (unless we agree for it to be a take-home test).

Course Objectives: to further your understanding of basic physics concept by learning how to apply them to solve a variety of problems. Both the conventional-type analytical problems (of the type encountered in graduate school entry-level Common Exams) as well as multiple-choice questions (found in GRE Physics tests) will be used. The scope of the material to be mastered (in both the 5020 and its predecessor, 5010, classes) roughly corresponds to the requirements of the GRE Physics Test (http://www.ets.org/gre/subject/about/content/physics), which the current American standard of proficiency for the undergraduate physics majors. Note that the 5010/5020 courses are foremost upper-level general physics classes and are not exactly GRE-preparation drills, so if you plan on using them to further your GRE preparation serious additional efforts are advised (such as real-time test taking practice, as well as the study of topics which are necessarily left out, such as laboratory techniques, particle physics, solid state, etc.)
