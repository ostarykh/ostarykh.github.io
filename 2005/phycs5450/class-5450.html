Course Description:

Physics 5450 represents a rigorous introduction to Quantum Mechanics. It begins by analyzing the most quantum object of nature - spin-1/2 particle - in the context of Stern-Gerlach experiment. From there follow such concepts as quantum state vectors, complex probability amplitudes, operators and their matrix representations, eigenvalues and eigenstates. Matrix mechanics connects with wave mechanics later in the course when we discuss the Schrodinger equation, perturbation theory and scattering.

Course PRE-REQUISITES: PHYCS 4420 & 3740, MATH 3150 & 3160

Required text: John S. Townsend, A Modern Approach to Quantum Mechanics, University Science Books, 2000;
ISBN 1-891389-13-0 ; (Library of Congress Catalog QC174.12.T69 2000).
Available in the University Bookstore and in the Mariott Library Reserve (2 books).

Additional Recommended Texts:

  • The Feynman Lectures on Physics, vol.3, Quantum Mechanics, Addison Wesley Longman (1970); ISBN 0201021153 . The classic and inspiring reading. Townsend's book is largely motivated by Feynman's.
  • Modern Quantum Mechanics, J.J. Sakurai, Addison Wesley Longman (1994); ISBN 0-201-53929-2 (this book is used in higher level quantum courses Phycs 7220 and 7230).
  • Quantum Mechanics (non-relativistic theory), L.D. Landau and E.M. Lifshitz, Butterworth-Heinemann, 3rd edition (1981); ISBN 0750635398 . A must for advanced theory students.
  • Introduction to Quantum Mechanics, D.J. Griffiths, Pearson Prentice Hall, 2nd edition (2005); ISBN 0-13-111892-7 . A very good book, used in many universities for quantum mechanics classes.

    Course Format:

    Class meets every day for 50 minutes. Lectures are given on Monday through Thursday. They are followed by Discussion Session on Friday, which is led by the Teaching Assistant. Discussion Session is organized to help you with the material covered in the preceeding lectures (usually this is done by working one-two problems from the textbook in details) and to answer your questions on homework problems.

    Homework:

    Weekly problem sets will be required and will count toward grades. Generally, we'll have one homework per week. Homework schedule can be found in the class Schedule which you should check regularly for posted homework assignments, additional suggested reading materials, as well as homework solutions which will be posted there every week.
  • Homeworks are to be turned in on Mondays, before 3 p.m.
    Discussions of homework problems are encouraged. However, every homework must be written individually. Copying other person's solutions is prohibited.
